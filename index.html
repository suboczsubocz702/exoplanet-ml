Gradient Boosting for Exoplanet Classification

Loss Function: Binary Cross-Entropy (Log Loss) Base Learner: Custom Decision Tree Regressor Accuracy: 0.90 +- 0.02

1. Problem Formulation

Let \( \mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^N \) denote the dataset where \( \mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^m \) represents astrophysical parameters (SNR, depth, duration, prad, period, impact) and engineered features, and \( y_i \in \{0,1\} \) indicates whether the signal corresponds to a true exoplanet (\(y_i=1\)) or a false positive (\(y_i=0\)).

The goal is to learn a function \( F: \mathbb{R}^m \rightarrow [0,1] \) that minimizes the expected log-loss:

\[ \mathcal{L}(F) = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \left[ y_i \log(F(\mathbf{x}_i)) + (1-y_i)\log(1-F(\mathbf{x}_i)) \right] \]

2. Gradient Boosting Algorithm

Gradient Boosting builds an additive model in a stage-wise fashion:

\[ F_M(\mathbf{x}) = F_0(\mathbf{x}) + \sum_{m=1}^M \eta \cdot h_m(\mathbf{x}) \]

where \( F_0(\mathbf{x}) = \log\left(\frac{\bar{y}}{1-\bar{y}}\right) \) is the initial prediction (log-odds of the prior), \( \eta \) is the learning rate, and \( h_m \) is a weak learner (decision tree) fitted to the negative gradient of the loss function.

2.1 Gradient Computation

At iteration \( m \), for each sample \( i \), the pseudo-residuals are:

\[ r_{i,m} = -\left[ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}(y_i, F(\mathbf{x}_i))}{\partial F(\mathbf{x}_i)} \right]_{F=F_{m-1}} = y_i - \frac{1}{1+e^{-F_{m-1}(\mathbf{x}_i)}} \]

These residuals represent the error between the true label and the predicted probability from the previous model.

2.2 Decision Tree Regressor

Each weak learner \( h_m \) is a regression tree that partitions the feature space into \( J \) terminal regions \( \{R_{j,m}\}_{j=1}^J \). For each region, the optimal update is computed by solving:

\[ \gamma_{j,m} = \underset{\gamma}{\text{argmin}} \sum_{\mathbf{x}_i \in R_{j,m}} \mathcal{L}\left(y_i, F_{m-1}(\mathbf{x}_i) + \gamma\right) \]

For log-loss, this reduces to a Newton-Raphson approximation:

\[ \gamma_{j,m} = \frac{\sum_{\mathbf{x}_i \in R_{j,m}} r_{i,m}}{\sum_{\mathbf{x}_i \in R_{j,m}} p_{i,m-1}(1-p_{i,m-1})} \]

where \( p_{i,m-1} = \sigma(F_{m-1}(\mathbf{x}_i)) \) is the predicted probability.

3. Feature Engineering

Domain knowledge from astrophysics guides the construction of interaction features:

\( f_7 = \frac{\text{depth}}{\text{duration}} \)
\( f_8 = \frac{\text{SNR}}{\text{period}} \)
\( f_9 = \text{depth} \times \text{duration} \)
\( f_{10} = \frac{\text{prad}}{\text{period}} \)
\( f_{11} = \frac{\text{duration}}{\text{period}} \)
\( f_{12} = \log(\text{SNR}) \)
\( f_{13} = \log(\text{depth}) \)

These transformations capture physical relationships such as the impact parameter scaling and signal-to-noise dependence on orbital geometry.

4. Training Dynamics

The model optimizes log-loss over \( M = 300 \) boosting iterations with learning rate \( \eta = 0.5 \). REgularization is achieved via tree depth constraints (max depth = 3) and sybsampling (feature bagging). The convergence is monitored through validation loss to prevent overfitting

Exoplanet Detection: The Transit Method

1. Physical Principles of Transit Photometry

When an exoplanet crosses (transits) the disk of its host star, it causes a periodic decrease in stellar flux. The fractional flux change \( \delta \) is directly related to the planet-to-star radius ratio:

\[ \delta = \frac{\Delta F}{F} = \left(\frac{R_p}{R_*}\right)^2 \]

where \( R_p \) is the planetary radius and \( R_* \) is the stellar radius. This forms the fundamental basis for radius determination.

2. Transit Geometry and Parameters

The observed transit light curve encodes several key physical parameters:

2.1 Impact Parameter

\[ b = \frac{a \cos i}{R_*} = \frac{d}{R_*} \]

where \( a \) is the orbital semi-major axis, \( i \) is the orbital inclination, and \( d \) is the projected distance from the star's center. For \( b > 1 \), no transit occurs.

2.2 Transit Duration

The total transit duration from first to fourth contact is:

\[ T_{\text{total}} = \frac{P}{\pi} \arcsin\left(\frac{R_*}{a}\sqrt{1-b^2}\right) \approx \frac{P}{\pi}\frac{R_*}{a}\sqrt{1-b^2} \]

where \( P \) is the orbital period. For a central transit (\( b=0 \)), this simplifies to \( T \approx \frac{P}{\pi}\frac{R_*}{a} \).

2.3 Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)

The SNR of a transit detection depends on the depth \( \delta \), the number of observed transits \( n \), and the photometric precision \( \sigma \):

\[ \text{SNR} = \frac{\delta \sqrt{n}}{\sigma} = \left(\frac{R_p}{R_*}\right)^2 \frac{\sqrt{n}}{\sigma} \]

3. Kepler Mission Data

The Kepler Space Telescope monitored ~150,000 stars with 30-minute cadence, achieving photometric precision of ~20 ppm for bright stars. The data reduction pipeline produces the following observables used in our model:

  • SNR: Combined signal significance across multiple transits
  • Depth (\( \delta \)): Primary flux decrement in parts per million (ppm)
  • Duration (\( T_{\text{total}} \)): Transit length in hours
  • Planet-to-star radius ratio (\( R_p/R_* \)): Derived from depth
  • Orbital period (\( P \)): Time between successive transits
  • Impact parameter (\( b \)): Inferred from light curve shape

4. False Positive Scenarios

Approximately 90% of Kepler candidates are false positives arising from:

  • Eclipsing Binaries (EBs): Grazing or background eclipsing binaries mimicking planetary transits
  • Stellar Variability: Pulsations, starspots, or activity cycles
  • Instrumental Artifacts: Data systematics and CCD anomalies
  • Hierarchical Triples: Diluted eclipses from multiple star systems

Our classifier leverages the distinctive signatures in parameter space—for instance, EBs often show V-shaped transit profiles (high impact parameter) and depth variations that differ from planetary transits.

5. Feature Importance from Physics

The engineered features are grounded in physical scaling relations:

  • depth/duration: Approximates the ingress/egress slope, sensitive to limb darkening and impact parameter
  • SNR/period: Balances detection significance against orbital architecture
  • depth x duration: Correlates with the total integrated signal, proportional to \( R_p^2 / (a \sqrt{1-b^2}) \)
